The Shape of Words

Typefaces and why you should care (at least a little.)

  • share
  • design
  • typography
  • dear client

Typography, the choice and appearance of content through the use of fonts, can make your break your site. It might seem like a non issue, as we consume written information without even thinking about it, but in each case the typeface used to convey that information was chosen with intention. From the serif fonts of the New York Times, to the clear and sparse didactics next to your favorite painting, a designer considered the purpose of the communication and selected a font accordingly.

Designing type is a discipline all its own. With foundations in graphic design and calligraphy, most type designers are hired to design an alphabet with a specific purpose. A great example of this is Georgia, a common serif typeface that was designed by Matthew Carter for Microsoft. Intended as the first serif screen font, a companion to the existing Verdana typeface, it was designed to appear larger on the screen than similar looking fonts of the same size (like Times New Roman) and was more readable at smaller sizes. Georgia went on to become a core web font, available on any browser.

Just as typefaces are designed for specific outputs, like screen or paper, they are also designed to communicate certain attitudes and emotions. Although it's something that few think about, consider the typefaces on your tax form. Helvetica, practically the poster child for modern typography is everywhere; it's on your tax form, transit signage of most major cities, and has been used in logotypes for CNN, NBC, Microsoft, Staples, 3M, and American Airlines. Being such a part of popular visual culture, Helvetica has grown to communicate modern, generic, authority. That impression can then be manipulated by brands and either celebrated in the Gap logo as an announcement of all American norms, or used subversively as American Apparel does, poking fun at corporate culture with its' scantily clad models. Either way, it's the type that's talking.
